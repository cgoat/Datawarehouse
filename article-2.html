Part 2: From Warehouse to Knowledge Graph — Teaching Gold to Speak

Continuing the spec-driven TPC-DS build: an ontology, a knowledge graph in Postgres, an Ollama-backed Q&A API, and a Neo4j visualization. Plus the silent, one-character bug that nearly ate the whole demo.

Part 2 of a hands-on write-up from the front lines · Read Part 1

Where we left off

In Part 1, an empty folder became a running Bronze/Silver/Gold warehouse on Postgres — 25 TPC-DS tables ingested, 48 dbt models generated, four Gold marts for the Business Analyst, and a dashboard for the Reliability Engineer. Four phases, 130 dbt nodes, sub-10-ms Gold queries. The CEO gets her data by 7 AM; the BA gets mart_customer_360; the RE gets his live health view.

And then I did what you always do after shipping a warehouse. I stared at it. And I thought: who actually uses this?

The honest answer is: a small number of analysts who know SQL, know the join keys, and have the patience to navigate a star schema from memory. Everyone else — the line-of-business person, the product manager, the executive who just wants to ask "which customers in California bought most from us last quarter?" — they don't touch it. Not because the data isn't there. Because the interface isn't there.

So I added two more phases to the roadmap.

  • Phase 4 — Ontology and Knowledge Graph. Define what the warehouse is about — in plain, business-shaped classes and relationships — and let a local LLM translate questions into SQL.
  • Phase 5 — Display the knowledge graph in Neo4j. Because a graph you can't see is just another schema diagram in the corner of a wiki no one visits.

What follows is the story of those two phases. Same spec-driven method, same AI pair programmer, same Windows machine. One silent bug that cost me an hour and made an excellent war story. And a second installment that — like the first — ended with me more convinced of the pattern, not less.

Why add a knowledge graph on top of a warehouse?

The star schema is a beautiful thing for analytics. It's a miserable thing for exploration. Tell me, from mart_customer_360 alone, how a customer connects to their household's income band. You can't; you need to join through dim_household_demographics and dim_income_band. Tell me which items are co-purchased by the highest-LTV customers. You'll be writing a subquery and a window function.

An ontology makes that implicit structure explicit. It says: a Customer lives at an Address. A Customer has Demographics. A Customer is in a Household. A Household is in an Income Band. A Customer purchased an Item. These aren't new facts — they're already in the foreign keys. But naming them, in business language, turns the schema into a thing a non-SQL person can reason about.

And once you have an ontology, three good things become possible:

  1. You can materialize it as a knowledge graph — nodes and edges — and traverse it the way humans actually think.
  2. You can hand that ontology to an LLM as context, and watch it generate useful SQL from plain English.
  3. You can visualize it — really visualize it — and suddenly people who never opened pgAdmin in their life are pointing at clusters and asking real questions.

That's the thesis. Phase 4 delivered (1) and (2). Phase 5 delivered (3).

Phase 4 — The ontology

I started where I always start now: a planning conversation, not code. What should the classes be? What should the relationships be? The warehouse has 17 dimensions and 7 fact tables; I didn't need all of them as first-class graph citizens, but I did need the ones a business user would recognize.

We landed on 15 classes — Customer, Item, Store, Warehouse, CallCenter, WebSite, WebPage, CatalogPage, Promotion, ShipMode, Reason, Address, Demographics, HouseholdDemographics, IncomeBand — and seven relationships that split neatly into two categories:

Foreign-key edges (1:1 structural): LIVES_AT, HAS_DEMOGRAPHICS, IN_HOUSEHOLD, IN_INCOME_BAND. These are the join keys already on the dimension tables — the ontology just gives them business names.

Aggregate edges (summarized fact): PURCHASED, SHOPPED_AT, RETURNED. Each comes out of a GROUP BY over an intermediate fact table, keeps the top-N pairs per source node, and carries aggregate properties on the edge itself — net_paid, order_count, quantity.

The whole definition lives in a single JSON file (knowledge_graph/ontology.json) that looks like this:

{
  "classes": {
    "Customer": {
      "source_table": "silver.dim_customer",
      "surrogate_key": "c_customer_sk",
      "natural_key": "c_customer_id",
      "label_expr": "coalesce(c_first_name,'') || ' ' || coalesce(c_last_name,'')",
      "properties": [
        {"name": "email", "column": "c_email_address"},
        {"name": "birth_year", "column": "c_birth_year"},
        ...
      ]
    },
    ...
  },
  "relationships": {
    "PURCHASED": {
      "from": "Customer", "to": "Item", "kind": "aggregate",
      "source": "silver.int_sales_unified",
      "from_column_sk": "customer_sk", "to_column_sk": "item_sk",
      "aggregates": {
        "order_count": "count(distinct channel || '|' || order_number)",
        "quantity":    "sum(quantity)",
        "net_paid":    "sum(net_paid)"
      },
      "top_n_per_from": 5
    },
    ...
  }
}

The ontology is the spec. The loader, the API, the PHP chat page, and eventually the Neo4j push all read from this one file. Change a class, everything downstream picks it up.

Phase 4 — Materializing the graph in Postgres

Why Postgres? Because the warehouse was already there, the data was already there, and introducing a second datastore at this stage would have cost more than it bought. A graph is an adjacency list; Postgres handles that fine.

I created a graph schema with five tables — ontology_class, ontology_relationship, node, edge, build_runs — and wrote a loader that reads ontology.json, upserts the metadata, iterates the classes to emit one node per dimension row, then iterates the relationships to emit edges.

The edge loader is where it got interesting. FK edges are easy — a join. Aggregate edges use a CTE that groups the fact table by (from_sk, to_sk), computes the aggregates, and optionally applies a per-source-node top-N so the graph doesn't balloon:

WITH agg AS (
  SELECT customer_sk AS from_sk, item_sk AS to_sk,
         count(distinct channel || '|' || order_number) AS order_count,
         sum(quantity)  AS quantity,
         sum(net_paid)  AS net_paid
    FROM silver.int_sales_unified
   WHERE customer_sk IS NOT NULL AND item_sk IS NOT NULL
   GROUP BY customer_sk, item_sk
),
ranked AS (
  SELECT *, row_number() OVER (PARTITION BY from_sk ORDER BY net_paid DESC NULLS LAST) AS rn
    FROM agg
)
INSERT INTO graph.edge (...)
SELECT fn.node_id, tn.node_id, 'PURCHASED', jsonb_build_object(...)
  FROM ranked src
  JOIN graph.node fn ON fn.class_name = 'Customer' AND fn.source_sk = src.from_sk
  JOIN graph.node tn ON tn.class_name = 'Item'     AND tn.source_sk = src.to_sk
 WHERE src.rn <= 5
ON CONFLICT (from_node_id, to_node_id, relationship_name) DO UPDATE
  SET properties = EXCLUDED.properties;

One build, against the existing Silver layer, produced:

Graph in Postgres
  • 2,108,206 nodes across 15 classes.
  • 1,245,212 edges across 7 relationships.
  • Biggest class: Demographics (1.92 M). Biggest relationship: PURCHASED (494 K).
  • Build took under two minutes. Idempotent — the ON CONFLICT DO UPDATE clauses mean you can re-run after every pipeline refresh.

I'll admit: the first time this ran end-to-end without error, I closed my laptop and walked outside for ten minutes. Two million nodes and a million-plus edges, from a single python -m knowledge_graph.build_graph invocation, against a warehouse the agent built from a roadmap bullet the same afternoon. That's the whole SDD pitch in one command.

Phase 4 — The LLM on top

A graph in Postgres is already useful for analysts. But the pitch here was plain English. For that I needed an LLM that generates SQL against graph.node and graph.edge, executes it read-only, and summarizes the result.

I wanted it local. No API key, no per-token cost, no data leaving the machine. Ollama was the obvious choice. The architecture is almost boring:

PHP chat page ──POST {question}──> Python HTTP API ──> Ollama (local) │ │ │ └── returns JSON { sql, rationale } │ ├── validate SQL (SELECT-only, whitelist schemas, no semicolons) ├── execute against Postgres (graph.* + silver + gold) ├── send rows back to Ollama for NL summary └── return { sql, rows, answer } to PHP

No Flask, no Django, no agent framework — just http.server from stdlib, urllib.request to reach Ollama, the existing psycopg connection for the warehouse. About 300 lines. The SQL validator is the most important 20: it accepts only queries that start with SELECT or WITH, rejects anything containing a semicolon or a forbidden keyword (INSERT, UPDATE, DELETE, DROP, …), and requires a reference to one of the whitelisted schemas (graph, silver, gold). If the LLM forgets a LIMIT, one is appended.

And then I spent the next hour and a half fighting a sequence of sharp edges that had nothing to do with my code.

Surprise 1: the port was "in use" — except it wasn't

First launch: PermissionError: [WinError 10013] An attempt was made to access a socket in a way forbidden by its access permissions.

I know this error. It almost always means the port is reserved by the Hyper-V / WSL / Docker "excluded port range" on Windows 11. Except netsh interface ipv4 show excludedportrange protocol=tcp said 8088 wasn't in the excluded set. Get-NetTCPConnection -LocalPort 8088 said nothing was listening. But socket.bind(('127.0.0.1', 8088)) returned EADDRINUSE anyway.

Windows networking is a layered operating system inside another operating system, and apparently some layer had reserved 8088 without telling the layers I know how to interrogate. I probed ports with a short Python loop, picked 8089 (which bound fine), and moved on. The fix in the code was a better error message so the next time this happens, the log tells the operator exactly what to do.

Surprise 2: Ollama returned 404 on a model I hadn't pulled

Second launch: Ollama unreachable at http://localhost:11434: HTTP Error 404: Not Found.

The model default in my config was llama3.1. My machine had qwen3.5:latest, gemma4:26b, and gemma4:latest. Ollama returns 404 on /api/generate when the model isn't local, which is a user-hostile error code but fine once you know it.

I made /health report model_present and the list of installed models, so misconfiguration is obvious before you submit a question. This is a small thing that would have saved me five minutes; worth the three lines.

Surprise 3: qwen3.5 returned an empty response

Third launch, OLLAMA_MODEL=qwen3.5:latest: the API returned could not parse SQL from LLM output: ''.

I called Ollama directly to see what the model was actually producing. It returned an empty string with eval_count: 23 — meaning 23 tokens were generated, but the visible response was nothing. That's the signature of a thinking model whose <think>…</think> block consumed all the output when format: "json" was set.

A small thinking model with format: "json" set produces a perfectly valid empty object. The model thought. It just didn't say anything.

Switched to gemma4:latest. Response came back in a second, parseable.

Surprise 4: small-model text-to-SQL is a humbling experience

Asked: "How many nodes are in each class in the knowledge graph?" Got back, verbatim:

SELECT class_name, COUNT(node_id) AS node_count
  FROM graph.node
 GROUP BY class_name
 ORDER BY node_count DESC
 LIMIT 200

Ran perfectly. 15 rows. Natural-language summary: "The knowledge graph contains the following number of nodes in each class, ordered by count descending: Demographics 1,920,800; Customer 100,000; Address 50,000; ...". I may have done a small fist-pump.

Asked: "Show the top 5 customers by total PURCHASED net_paid." Got back, verbatim:

SELECT ..., SUM(T2.properties->>'net_paid') AS total
  FROM ...

Postgres, justifiably, replied: function sum(text) does not exist. The model had extracted the JSONB field as text and tried to sum text. This is a well-known text-to-SQL pitfall; the fix is in the system prompt, not the code. I added an explicit rule ("BEFORE aggregating a value from jsonb, cast it: (e.properties->>'net_paid')::numeric") and — when that alone wasn't enough — a few-shot example of the exact pattern.

On the next run the model produced:

SELECT c.label AS customer_name,
       SUM((e.properties->>'net_paid')::numeric) AS total_net_paid
  FROM graph.node c
  JOIN graph.edge e ON e.from_node_id = c.node_id
 WHERE c.class_name = 'Customer'
   AND e.relationship_name = 'PURCHASED'
 GROUP BY c.node_id, c.label
 ORDER BY total_net_paid DESC
 LIMIT 5

Results:

CustomerTotal net paid
Wayne Land$63,707.78
Jamel Owen$50,998.74
Sheila Stover$50,188.79
Jesus Tilley$49,819.85
Julie (no last name)$49,454.50

The same answer Phase 2 would have produced with a three-table join and a SUM. But I didn't write the join — the LLM did, from an ontology file and an English sentence.

When a local LLM, running on the same machine, writes correct warehouse SQL from plain English and hands you back a ranked list — you realize the interface layer has quietly moved.

Phase 5 — Display the graph

Phase 4 gave me a knowledge graph and a way to query it. What it didn't give me was a way to see it. A chat UI is useful but it isn't a map. So Phase 5 was straightforward in scope: put the graph in Neo4j, so I could browse it visually at localhost:7474.

I picked Docker for the install path. Neo4j as a Windows service is a download-installer-restart dance that I don't want to impose on a Medium reader. Neo4j in Docker is:

docker compose -f infra/docker-compose.neo4j.yml up -d

And the Browser is live two minutes later, with the password coming from .env like everything else in the project.

The loader — knowledge_graph/push_to_neo4j.py — reads graph.node and graph.edge from Postgres, connects to Neo4j over Bolt, and pushes in UNWIND-MERGE batches. Every node gets a generic :Entity label (for the uniqueness constraint) plus a per-class label (:Customer, :Item, …) so the Browser colors them nicely. Relationships keep their ontology names: PURCHASED, LIVES_AT, SHOPPED_AT.

Two modes: --sample (default) pushes a bounded subset — top-N customers plus their outbound 2-hop neighbors, about 1,700 nodes — small enough that the Browser will actually render it. --full pushes everything, takes minutes, and is really only useful for Cypher queries rather than visualization.

The thing that made this phase memorable wasn't the code. It was the bug.

The silent bug

First push of the sample looked great. Log output:

[neo4j] sample: 1,715 nodes
[neo4j] nodes pushed: 1,715
[neo4j] edges pushed: 2,553
[neo4j] in database: 1,715 nodes, 1,559 relationships

Relationships-by-type check in Cypher:

RETURNED                   493
SHOPPED_AT                 287
LIVES_AT                   200
IN_HOUSEHOLD               192
HAS_DEMOGRAPHICS           191
IN_INCOME_BAND             190
PURCHASED                    6

Six. Six PURCHASED relationships, out of 1,000 that the Postgres query said were in the sample. The top-N-per-customer math said there should be roughly 1,000 — 200 customers, up to 5 items each — and Postgres confirmed 1,000 when I queried it directly. The Python iterator emitted 1,000 when I instrumented it. The Cypher MERGE statement got 1,000 rows in its parameter list. And only 6 survived.

I spent fifteen minutes convinced it was a MERGE-collapse problem — maybe the (from, to, type) triples weren't as unique as I thought. Counted them. 1,000 unique triples. Not that.

Then I pulled a single row out of the Postgres output: a PURCHASED edge from Customer 2064 to Item 42. Checked Customer 2064 in Neo4j — present, labeled :Customer, source_sk: 2064. Checked Item 42 in Neo4j:

Item source_sk=42: {'label': 'ableese', 'class': 'pop', 'source_sk': 42}
by class+source_sk: 0

There's the Item. But the match by class: 'Item', source_sk: 42 returned zero rows. Because the class property was 'pop', not 'Item'.

Slow, reluctant dawning. pop. That's the Item's TPC-DS category. i_class — the column I'd included as a node property back in the ontology — had just overwritten the class identifier I was using for Neo4j MERGE matching. Every Item in Neo4j had its identifier field silently rewritten to the Item's category. And the edge MATCH — keyed on class: 'Item' — was failing on 994 of the 1,000 PURCHASED rows, because those items' class property now said "sports" or "home" or "men's pants".

The fix was one word: rename my internal identifier from class to node_class, so the Item's domain property can't collide with the graph's structural property. One line of the Cypher template. One extra line in _apply_constraints to drop the obsolete constraint.

Re-run: 1,715 nodes, 2,553 relationships, 1,000 PURCHASED.

Why this bug was dangerous Everything succeeded. Every query ran without error. Every batch returned. The loader said "edges pushed: 2,553". The Neo4j database said "2,553 relationships" on the first run (before I tightened and re-ran) — wait, no, it said 1,559. Because the Cypher MATCHes for the 994 broken rows found no nodes and silently created no relationships. Cypher's MATCH-then-MERGE does not warn you when the MATCH finds nothing; it just does nothing. The count was off by 994 with no error anywhere in any log.

The only reason I caught it is that I eyeballed the relationship breakdown. If I hadn't, I would have shipped a graph that looked fine and was quietly missing 40% of its most important edges.
Domain data has no respect for your schema conventions. If your identifier field name can collide with a user property, it will, and the failure will be silent.

The other two surprises in Phase 5 were less dramatic:

A naive wipe (MATCH (n) DETACH DELETE n) hit Neo4j's per-transaction memory pool (716 MB) once the graph was non-trivial. The Neo4j 5 pattern is MATCH (n) CALL (n) { DETACH DELETE n } IN TRANSACTIONS OF 2000 ROWS — auto-commits per batch, fits in memory, works on any size graph.

My first sample expansion was over-generous. I'd included the small dims (Store, CatalogPage, Warehouse, …) in the sample seed plus done a 1-hop expansion. Which meant: a single Store seed pulled in every customer who had ever shopped at that store. Sample size: 120,000 nodes. The Browser would have rendered the first 300 and called it a day. Restricted expansion to outbound-only-from-top-customers; sample dropped to 1,715, which is what the Browser can actually display.

What landed — the numbers

End-to-end numbers
  • Ontology: 15 classes, 7 relationships, 213 lines of JSON.
  • Knowledge graph in Postgres: 2,108,206 nodes and 1,245,212 edges from silver.*.
  • Python KG API: stdlib-only HTTP server, ~330 lines. Read-only SQL validator (SELECT/WITH-only, schema-whitelisted, no semicolons, auto-LIMIT).
  • PHP chat UI: one file, 170 lines. Sidebar shows live class/relationship counts pulled from graph.ontology_class.
  • Ollama model: gemma4:latest, fully local, no keys, no egress.
  • Neo4j: 5.26 Community in Docker. Sample push: 1,715 nodes / 2,553 relationships. Full push: all 2.1 M / 1.2 M, minutes-scale.
  • Bugs caught during Phase 4/5: five — the stale port reservation, the missing Ollama model, the empty qwen output, the text-to-SQL cast gap, and the class-property collision. Every one of them silent-ish; every one of them found by eyeballing output.

The demo flow is short. Open the PHP page. Type: "Which stores are in CA?" Watch gemma4 generate a 4-line query against graph.node, execute it, and return six California stores with a natural-language summary. Click over to localhost:7474. Paste MATCH (c:Customer)-[p:PURCHASED]->(i:Item) RETURN c, p, i LIMIT 50. Watch a constellation of 50 customer-to-item edges render in the Browser. Click a node; the Browser expands its neighborhood. Suddenly your warehouse is a map.

What I took away from Phases 4 and 5

Three things that I expect to carry into the next warehouses in this series.

1. The ontology is the spec that outlasts the storage. I have the same ontology living in two places now: materialized in Postgres as graph.node/graph.edge, and materialized in Neo4j as labeled nodes and typed relationships. If I add Snowflake next, the ontology moves over untouched. The source tables change; the business meaning doesn't. This is the first time I've felt like a "spec" actually earned its keep as an artifact you port.

2. Local LLMs are good enough to be useful, and small enough to have sharp edges. gemma4:latest is not GPT-5, and it shouldn't be. It runs on my laptop, it costs zero dollars per query, and it doesn't leave the machine. Give it a well-scoped ontology, a JSON-only output contract, a few-shot example, and a whitelist validator that catches its mistakes, and it produces correct SQL against a warehouse schema it has never seen before. The moment the ecosystem produces a 7B-class model that doesn't need the few-shot on ::numeric casts, this pattern gets dramatically easier.

3. Silent failures are the expensive ones. The class-property collision was the most expensive bug of the two phases, and it never raised an exception anywhere in the stack. Cypher's MATCH returning zero rows is not an error. The Python loader's "edges pushed: 2,553" is accurate — 2,553 UNWIND rows were sent. The count drop happened entirely inside Neo4j's MERGE semantics, without a log line, without a warning, without even a statistic that flagged it. The only defense is verification: compare output to expectation, aggressively, at every layer. This is not a new lesson, but it is one I keep relearning.

The experience again was that the AI pair programmer earned its seat. It proposed the split between FK edges and aggregate edges; it chose jsonb over columns-per-property; it caught my initial 120 K-node sample size for what it was ("over-expansion through small dims"); it asked whether I wanted --full to even exist given what would happen to the Browser. When the class-collision bug showed up, it didn't find the bug — I did, by eye — but it executed the exact diagnostic sequence (check Postgres output, check iterator output, spot-check a specific node in Neo4j) that let me narrow it down in under fifteen minutes. That's what "pair programmer" means now: not autocomplete, but a second set of disciplined hands on the debugger.

The series continues

Two phases down on this warehouse. Zero clean-up needed to justify adding them — both shipped behind the existing dashboard, both reviewable in PRs, both runnable with three batch scripts each. The ontology lives in a JSON file that can be re-read every time I want to re-materialize against a different storage layer.

Next up is what Part 1 promised: Snowflake. The cost model is different, the partitioning is different, the tooling is different, and the "warehouse" concept lands in a completely different place than it does on Postgres.
